# Bank-Stress-Test-Simulator
Stress Testing system for Banking Sector With Tanzania as the case study
Tanzania’s banks are the main source of funding for farming, small businesses, and the informal economy. They hold about 72% of all financial assets and turn deposits into loans and The 10 largest banks account for about 79% of total assets, making the system heavily reliant on major commercial players as described by (Tanzania Invest, 2025)
In recent years, several risks have built up. Droughts and El Ni no threaten farm loans, and income shocks make it harder for informal and micro-borrowers to repay. Bad loans in farm and small-business lending are around 40% higher than the banking average. At the same time, the Tanzanian shilling has weakened losing about 9–10% against the US dollar in 2024–25
hurting borrowers with foreign-currency debts. These pressures can quickly eat into banks’ capital by raising bad loans and the money banks must set aside for losses as per(Bank of Tanzania, 2024.)This danger was clear between 2016 and 2018, when eight small community banks were closed or liquidated after bad loans spiked. By late 2017, the industry’s non-performing loan ratio
reached about 11.5%, more than double what it was two years earlier. The (International Monetary Fund, 2018) review noted worsening loan quality and underdeveloped stress-testing.In response, the Bank of Tanzania tightened rules. Banks must now hold a minimum total capital adequacy ratio of 12%, and new stress-testing guidelines based on Basel III were issued in 2022 by Bank of Tanzania, (2022.) 
These changes helped: by the end of 2024, the bad-loan ratio had fallen to about 3–4%, and most banks’ capital ratios were well above the 12% minimum. Still, serious risks remain in severe scenarios. Climate and currency shocks could still cause loan losses to pile up quickly. Experts also note that banks and regulators need stronger practical skills in stress testing. This creates a clear need for simple,
scenario-based simulation tools that show how sudden shocks to bad loans or exchange rates would affect each bank’s capital and liquidity.

Tanzanian commercial banks face serious risks that can quickly weaken their finances and threaten overall stability. Climate shocks like droughts raise bad loans among farming and small-business borrowers, defaults in micro loans when a borrower completely fails to repay their small, short-term loan according to the agreed schedule. in the informal sector add further strain, and currency depreciation also hurts 
banks with foreign exchange exposures. These interconnected pressures can rapidly erode the banks capital. Between 2016 and 2018, eight banks collapsed because too many loans weren’t repaid. At the worst point, more than 11 out of every 100 loans were not being repaid, and some banks had over 30 bad loans out of 100 as stated by Nsubuga, (2022).  A 2018 check by the IMF found that the safety rules and stress tests 
at the time were not strong enough to spot these dangers early as stated by International Monetary Fund, (2018) Today, bad loans have fallen to about 3 in 100 and banks hold more safety money, but the same risks from farming and currency loss of value remain. Stress testing tools are not fully developed, leaving a dangerous gap.
Due to ongoing risks in the droughts scenarios and other risks in non performing loans which can be considered when timely payments are not done within 90 days, there is a need for a stress testing tool to add hypothetical scenarios to measure how the overall health of the bank can be if such extreme conditions were to happen this helps project how the bank can sustain such financial crisis.
